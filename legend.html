pre-ramyana: A Tale of Legend

Act I – The Rise of Zero

Chapter 1: The Whisper Before Birth

The world was young, yet ancient. It was the age before the coming of Rama, when time itself seemed to move slowly, as if savoring the breath of creation. The forests were vast and deep, filled with secrets older than kings, older than the memory of mankind. Mountains pierced the sky like the teeth of sleeping giants, and rivers slithered through the land like silver serpents, carrying whispers from the heavens.

Sages sat in meditation beneath banyan trees, their chants rising into the ether like sparks of fire, holding the balance between light and darkness. Gods sometimes descended in radiant forms to test mortals, while demons crept in the shadows, forever seeking to twist destiny. It was a world where the strong were worshipped, and the weak were forgotten.

In this land, every child’s birth was celebrated with the expectation of greatness. Families prayed for warriors who could wield bows that shook mountains, for singers whose voices could summon rain, for healers whose touch could cure plagues. The belief was clear: a child without talent was not only worthless, but cursed.

The Village of Hope

At the edge of the Dandaka forest lay a small village called Madhavpur, a settlement of humble farmers, hunters, and traders. Though it was small, its fame was not, for many who were born here carried gifts that made the land proud. Sons of Madhavpur had joined armies of great kings, daughters had sung in the courts of sages, and some had even walked the path of seers.

The people of Madhavpur were fiercely proud, for they believed their soil was blessed. When a child was born, elders gathered to read omens. Stars were studied, fire crackled in sacred rites, and the air carried whispers of the future.

But on one storm-filled night, a birth occurred that would shake the very foundations of this faith.

The Child with No Mark

The hut was small, built of mud and thatch. Inside, the air was thick with the smell of rain-soaked earth. A young woman lay upon a straw mat, her face pale with the strain of childbirth. Beside her, midwives worked with hurried hands, their chants mingling with the thunder outside.

And then, the child came. A boy. His first cry was thin, almost fragile, unlike the strong wails that usually filled huts with pride.

The midwives exchanged uneasy glances. They waited for signs: perhaps a sudden glow in the fire, a breeze carrying fragrance, a bird’s call breaking the storm. But nothing came. The boy lay silent, ordinary. No divine aura kissed his skin, no mystic symbols marked his flesh. His eyes—when they opened—were wide and blank, as though holding no memory of heaven.

One of the midwives whispered, “No sign… no mark… no blessing.”

Another muttered, “A hollow birth. This child carries nothing.”

The whispers spread quickly. Outside, villagers who had gathered for news began to murmur.

“Did the gods forget?”
“No star shone tonight.”
“Born empty. Born with nothing.”

A Mother’s Faith, A Father’s Doubt

Inside, the young mother cradled her child. Exhausted, yet her eyes glowed with a strange tenderness. She pressed the frail infant against her chest, whispering, “You are mine. You will not be nothing.”

But the father, Raghava, stood at a distance. His brow furrowed, not with joy but with unease. “A son without blessing will bring shame,” he muttered under his breath. He had hoped for a warrior, or at least a child with some gift to raise their family’s standing. Instead, he saw only weakness.

When the mother looked up, she said softly, “Do not judge him yet. Even the smallest seed may grow into the mightiest tree.”

Raghava did not answer. His silence was heavier than words.

Foreshadowing

The storm outside grew fiercer, but within the shadows of the village road, an old wandering sage appeared. His beard was long, his eyes sunken yet sharp as embers. No one knew his name, for he walked from land to land like a drifting flame.

He stepped into the hut without invitation, and all fell silent. His gaze fell upon the child.

For a long moment, he said nothing. Then he whispered words that would echo across time:

“Sometimes the one born with nothing… creates everything.”

The villagers scoffed. One laughed bitterly, “What can a hollow boy create? He cannot even cry with strength.”

The sage’s eyes narrowed, but he spoke no more. He turned, and with the storm at his back, he vanished into the night as swiftly as he had come.

The Name of Shunya

The villagers, however, were not silent. By morning, the boy had been given a name—not by his parents, but by the crowd.

They called him “Shunya” — Zero.

Children repeated it with cruel delight, elders shook their heads in pity, and fathers laughed that such a child would never lift a bow, never chant a mantra, never bring honor.

Only his mother held him close, whispering again and again, “You are not Shunya. You are my Anirudh. You are endless, not empty.”

But the world did not listen. To them, he was already a nothing.

Closing Scene

That night, as the rain ceased, the sky cleared. Stars glittered above like scattered jewels, but none seemed to shine for the child. He lay asleep in his mother’s arms, fragile, ordinary, forgotten.

Yet beyond the stars, in realms unseen by men, the gods watched. Some with pity, some with amusement, and a few with a strange, silent fear. For destiny had written a paradox: a boy born with nothing, who would one day rise above all.

And so began the tale of the one the world called Zero.
