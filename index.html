Child of the Crimson Pendant

INSIDE A SMALL HUT – NIGHT

The wind howled like a hungry wolf, tearing at the weak walls of the hut. Heavy rain beat down on the thatched roof, threatening to collapse it. Inside, the air was thick with the smell of wet earth and fear. A young woman, her face pale and streaked with tears, lay on a simple cot. Her breath was shallow, but her eyes were fixed on the small baby in her arms.

WOMAN

(whispering, her voice cracking) Anirudh... my little warrior. Forgive me. I cannot protect you anymore.

Her shaking fingers reached for a heavy copper chain around her neck. On it hung a round pendant, etched with a swirling, ancient symbol. As her fingers closed around it, the symbol pulsed with a soft, golden light, pushing back against the shadows in the hut.

With the last of her strength, she placed the chain over the baby’s head. The cool metal rested against his skin, and the pendant’s glow seemed to brighten, covering him in a protective light.

WOMAN (V.O.)

Your father’s blood flows in you. The blood of the Alchemist King. They will hunt you for it. But you must live... you must survive.

The storm raged, and the woman’s breath grew weaker. She held her baby, Anirudh, with all her might.
WOMAN (her voice a faint whisper) Don't let their darkness touch you... Carry the light... Find your strength...
A loud crash of thunder shook the hut, and the flimsy door was thrown open. A figure stood in the doorway, tall and completely hidden by a dark, heavy cloak. No face could be seen, only shadow, but a feeling of cold, deadly intent filled the small room. The woman gasped, her last hope gone. The cloaked figure stepped inside, and her world went black.

Act 1: The Spark

The Orphan of Kanjiram

FIFTEEN YEARS LATER - OUTSIDE KANJIRAM ACADEMY – MORNING

Fifteen years of hardship had forged Anirudh into a weapon. Now a young man of fifteen, his body was lean and powerful, every muscle defined by years of relentless, self-imposed training. His eyes, dark and intense, held a fire that seemed out of place in the peaceful morning. He wore simple, patched clothes, a stark contrast to the fine silks of the students who trained within the walls of the famous Kanjiram Academy.

He gripped a heavy wooden staff, its surface battered and splintered from countless strikes. He moved with a raw, explosive grace that no formal training could teach. This was the style of a survivor, of someone who had fought for everything. He attacked a thick oak tree as if it were a real enemy, his jaw set with a determination that was both inspiring and terrifying. He was not just practicing; he was preparing for war.

The heavy staff hit the tree with a loud WHUMP. Sweat dripped from Anirudh's face. Each swing was filled with a quiet, burning ambition.
VILLAGER (O.S.)
Hey! Still at it, orphan? You’ll break that tree before you get into that fancy academy!
Anirudh paused, a sharp, dangerous grin flashing across his face. The passing villager, a man with a face as weathered as old leather, flinched back almost imperceptibly. The boy's intensity was unsettling. Anirudh's words did not wound him; they were fuel to a fire that already burned brighter than the sun.
ANIRUDH
(grinning) Then I’ll break both.
He turned back to the tree, his grin vanishing, replaced by a focus so absolute it seemed to suck the sound from the air around him. The staff resumed its rhythmic assault.
A group of boys in fine, colorful silks walked by. At their lead was a boy with a sneering face and clothes so pristine they looked ridiculous on the dusty road. He pointed a manicured finger at Anirudh, his friends snickering behind him like a pack of hyenas.
BULLY BOY
(Voice dripping with scorn) Look who thinks he can join us! You? At Kanjiram Academy? Maybe to clean the stables, orphan!
Anirudh felt the familiar sting, but years of such treatment had forged an iron will within him. He refused to give them the satisfaction of a reaction. He simply met the bully's gaze, his own eyes holding a quiet fire that the other boy could not hope to extinguish.
ANIRUDH
One day, I’ll stand where the champions do. And you’ll be looking up at me.
His voice was calm, but held an intensity that silenced their laughter. He held the bully's gaze for a second longer, a silent promise, then turned back to his training as if they were nothing more than buzzing flies.
KANJIRAM ACADEMY – MAIN GATE – DAY

The academy's main gate was a colossal structure of dark stone, carved with symbols of power that seemed to warn away the unworthy. A long line of hopefuls stretched from it, a river of silk, polished leather, and nervous, privileged faces. They were the children of lords and merchants, their hands soft, their expressions arrogant.

Among them, Anirudh was a splinter of the real world. His clothes were patched but clean, his hands calloused from work and training. He ignored the whispers and disgusted looks from the other applicants, his gaze fixed on the gate. He belonged here, and he would prove it.

KANJIRAM ACADEMY – MAIN GATE – DAY

Anirudh reached the front of the line. Before him sat an instructor with a face like carved stone and eyes that missed nothing. The man's gaze swept over Anirudh's worn clothes with a flicker of disdain.

INSTRUCTOR
(In a harsh, bored voice) Name?
ANIRUDH
Anirudh.
He did not add "no last name." He let the single name hang in the air, a statement of its own. A ripple of gasps and sneering whispers went through the line. "An orphan." "Worthless." Anirudh stood unflinching, his focus entirely on the instructor, a silent challenge in his eyes.
INSTRUCTOR
(Looking him up and down) We’ll see if you survive the Trials. Step forward when called.

The whispers were a cloud of insects at his back, but Anirudh ignored them. Their scorn was a weapon, and he would turn it back on them. Let them see an orphan. Soon, they would see a warrior. He clutched the copper pendant beneath his tunic, its familiar weight a secret promise. He walked towards the waiting area, his shoulders straight, ready for the fire.

The Trials of Initiation

EXT. STONE ARENA – TRIAL FIELD – DAY

Fifty hopefuls were herded into the Stone Arena, a vast, circular pit carved from the earth, its walls scarred from generations of past trials. The air hummed with nervous energy and the silent judgment of the stern-faced instructors who stood like statues around the perimeter. This was the crucible, and it was about to be lit.

ANNOUNCER (O.S.)
"The Trial of Initiation begins! Prove your worth! Only the strongest, the fastest, the most resilient shall pass!"

The first challenge was a grueling test of endurance, an obstacle course designed to break the spirit. The privileged youths, who had never known a day of true labor, were the first to fall, collapsing in heaps of their fine silk clothes. But Anirudh moved with a relentless efficiency. He did not have their brute strength, but he had something better: a will of iron and a body that knew how to conserve every ounce of energy. He navigated the course not with speed, but with intelligence, each movement precise and purposeful. The pain from his scrapes and bruises was a familiar fire, one he had learned to use as fuel.

The second trial was one of agility: a treacherous landscape of rising and falling pillars over a deep pit. Here, the formally trained students tried to apply their rigid techniques and failed, their movements too predictable. Anirudh, however, moved with a feral instinct. His style was born from a life of dodging angry merchants and leaping across rooftops. He didn't think; he reacted, his body a blur of motion, his worn boots finding purchase on surfaces that should have been impossible to stand on. He moved like a creature of the wild, unpredictable and unstoppable.

The third test was of power. The goal: move a massive stone. The largest youths, full of pride, attempted the heaviest boulders and failed, their muscles giving out. Anirudh, ever the strategist, studied the stones. He chose a smaller one, but used his staff as a perfect lever and the slight incline of the ground to his advantage. It was a brilliant display of physics over brute force. With a great heave, he moved the stone farther than anyone else, proving that the mind is the greatest weapon. A ripple of stunned silence, then grudging admiration, passed through the onlookers.

From a high platform, two instructors watched. One was the stern-faced man from the gate. The other, a man with a sharp beard and intelligent eyes, was Master Elara. "He has the spirit of a survivor," Master Elara noted, his voice tinged with interest.

The stern instructor scoffed. "Grit alone does not make one worthy of Kanjiram."

Master Elara's eyes remained fixed on Anirudh. "Perhaps," he murmured to himself. "But a fire like that can forge a legend."

That night, unable to rest, Anirudh slipped into the academy's grand library. He was not looking for textbooks, but for answers. He searched the forbidden sections, his fingers tracing the spines of ancient, dust-covered tomes, seeking any clue about the strange, swirling symbol on the pendant he wore, his only link to a past he could not remember.

Days of brutal trials passed. The field of fifty hopefuls had been whittled down to a handful of exhausted, but determined, survivors. A great voice echoed across the arena.

ANNOUNCER (V.O.)

"The final trial is at hand! Only ten shall be granted entry into the esteemed Kanjiram Academy!"

The remaining students looked at each other, their camaraderie replaced by fierce rivalry. Anirudh’s expression, however, remained unchanged. His gaze was locked forward, his will absolute. He only needed one spot.

The final challenge was combat. A single figure emerged from the shadows of the arena gate. They were tall, cloaked, and their face was hidden by a featureless, black mask that seemed to drink the light. An aura of immense power and deadly skill radiated from them. It was a masked instructor, a master of the Kanjiram arts.

The other students hesitated, their courage failing them. But Anirudh, though weary, felt a surge of adrenaline. This was it. The ultimate test. He stepped forward without a single word, his worn boots crunching on the arena sand, and stood alone before the terrifying figure.

The masked instructor’s head tilted slightly. The voice that came from behind the mask was low and laced with contempt.

"You have lasted this long, orphan," the instructor said. "A surprise. But your journey ends here."

The insult, meant to unbalance him, had the opposite effect. It centered him. Anirudh's jaw tightened, and the fire in his eyes burned hotter.

For Anirudh, the word "orphan" was not an insult. It was a badge of honor. It was a testament to his unbreakable will to survive in a world that had given him nothing.

He straightened his shoulders, his voice clear and steady, ringing with a power that surprised even himself.

ANIRUDH

"Orphans learn to fight harder than anyone. We have everything to prove."

He was not just fighting for a place in an academy. He was fighting for his past, his future, and his very identity. He would not be broken.

The instructor attacked with blinding speed and precision. It was like fighting a whirlwind. Anirudh was instantly on the defensive, his staff a blur as he parried a furious storm of blows. He was outmatched in skill and technique, but not in spirit.

He grit his teeth, his mind racing. He could not win a conventional fight. He had to be unconventional. He had to be unpredictable. He had to be the survivor he had always been.

Anirudh abandoned the formal stances he had tried to learn. He began to move in a way that was uniquely his own—a messy, chaotic, and brilliant dance of survival. He ducked under a sweeping strike that should have taken his head off and, in the same motion, swept his leg out in a low, unexpected attack.

It was not a powerful move, but it was perfectly timed. It caught the instructor's ankle, breaking their perfect stance. The master stumbled. A wave of shock rippled through the crowd. The untouchable instructor had been hit.

The instructor, a master of formal combat, was completely unprepared for Anirudh's wild, street-honed style. The stumble was more than a physical misstep; it was a crack in their aura of invincibility. For the first time, the crowd saw not an orphan, but a contender. Whispers of awe replaced the earlier sneers.

The instructor, now furious, attacked with even greater intensity. But the dynamic of the fight had shifted. Anirudh had proven he was not some street rat to be easily crushed. He was a force to be reckoned with. The impossible had just happened, and everyone had seen it.

The instructor pressed their attack, but Anirudh was no longer just trying to win. He was trying to endure. He knew he could not defeat the master, but he could refuse to be defeated. Every block, every dodge, was a declaration of his will.

The seconds stretched into an eternity. The arena was silent, the crowd watching with bated breath as the orphan boy refused to fall. Then, the final bell rang, its clear note echoing through the arena.

The fight was over. Anirudh stood, bruised, battered, and trembling with exhaustion, but he was still standing. He had not won the fight, but he had won something far more important: respect.

The Tenth Place

Anirudh lay on the simple, hard bunk in the academy dormitory, the air thick with the smell of sweat and healing herbs. His body was a tapestry of bruises, each one a memory of the brutal trials. But beneath the physical pain, a quiet warmth spread through his chest. He had done it. He was one of the ten.

A voice, amplified by magic, echoed through the stone walls, reading the list of the chosen. The names were a roll call of the children of lords and wealthy merchants. Nine names were called. Anirudh held his breath, the silence stretching. Then, finally: "...Tenth. Anirudh."

His eyes snapped open, his heart soaring. The last spot. The one nobody thought he would get. It was his. A small, tired smile touched his lips. It was the smile of a boy who had been told he was worthless his entire life and had just proven the whole world wrong.

But victory felt strangely hollow. He was in the academy, but he was an island in a sea of privilege. The other students ignored him, their silence as sharp as their earlier insults. In the quiet of the night, he felt the familiar weight of being utterly alone.

His hand instinctively went to his neck, his fingers closing around the cool, heavy copper of the Crimson Pendant. It was his only link to a past he didn't understand, a silent promise that he was more than just an orphan. Getting into the academy wasn't the end of his fight. It was just the beginning.

The Forbidden Section

Weeks turned into months. Anirudh excelled in his physical training, his raw power and unorthodox style making him a formidable opponent. But the instructors were wary of him, and the other students feared him. He learned little of the true arts of Kanjiram, and nothing of his past. Frustrated, he began his own search for answers in the one place they might be found: the academy's vast library.

Late one night, he slipped into the forbidden section, a part of the library rumored to hold dangerous and cursed texts. The air here was cold and heavy with the scent of ancient paper and forgotten magic. He moved like a shadow through the towering shelves, his heart pounding with a mix of fear and determination.

His fingers traced the spines of the ancient books until he found it: a heavy, leather-bound tome with a symbol on its cover that matched the one on his pendant. With trembling hands, he opened it. The book spoke of the Alchemist King, of a powerful lineage, and of monstrous creatures called Wildblood Beasts that were drawn to the king's power. A chill went down his spine. The pendant wasn't just a memento. It was a key. And it made him a target.

Meanwhile, Across the Continent...

The story shifts to a different part of the world, to a trio of adventurers exploring ancient ruins.

Elara, a woman with eyes that seemed to see the hidden energy in the world, ran her hand over a set of strange carvings on a crumbling wall. She was a mage, her power tied to the ancient flows of magic in the earth. Beside her stood Kaelen, a stoic warrior in scarred leather armor, his hand resting on the hilt of a massive greatsword. He was her sworn protector, his loyalty as unshakeable as a mountain.

"These symbols are unlike any I've seen," Elara murmured, a frown on her face. "They feel... wrong."

As they ventured deeper, the ground began to glow with a faint, sickly crimson light. The air grew heavy, and a low, chanting sound seemed to rise from the stones themselves. "This is dark magic," Kaelen growled, drawing his sword. "The kind that taints the very soul of a place."

They entered a clearing where a group of cloaked figures were performing a ritual, their voices rising in a terrifying chorus. In the center of their circle, a shadowy creature was beginning to form, a being of pure malice and hunger.

Elara and Kaelen fought bravely, but they were outnumbered. The shadowy creature was immune to Kaelen's blade, and Elara's magic was being suppressed by the dark ritual. They were captured and thrown into a damp, lightless prison cell, the scent of blood and old magic thick in the air.

In the cell next to them, they were surprised to find another prisoner. He introduced himself as Elias, a scholar with a wiry frame, a sharp, intelligent face, and spectacles perched on his nose. He had been captured weeks ago while studying the ruins.

"They are trying to summon something," Elias explained in a hushed voice, his eyes wide with fear and academic curiosity. "Something connected to the old kings. They are searching for an heir... someone with the blood of the Alchemist King."

Elara and Kaelen exchanged a look. The world was larger and more dangerous than they had imagined, and it seemed all paths were leading to a single, unknown child.

A storm was gathering. Elias stood at the edge of a deep ravine. The journal he carried, his only link to his past, told him the next clue was on the other side. Though it was dangerous, he knew he had to cross it. He started to climb down into the darkness.

Elara and Kaelen found a small, carved wooden box on a pedestal. It hummed with ancient magic. As they got closer, the ground trembled, and a tall, shadowy figure appeared. "You intrude where you do not belong," a deep voice said. "The secrets of this place are not for mortals."

Elara woke up in a damp prison cell. She didn't know how she got there. She heard a groan from the next cell and was relieved to find her friend Kael was alive. But they were trapped, and they didn't know where their other friends were, or what their captors wanted.

Elara and Kael were climbing a dangerous mountain. The path was narrow, and a single misstep could mean death. The ancient symbols carved into the rock around them seemed to glow. They were close to the summit and whatever secret it held.

Elara and Caelan entered a large cavern. In the center was a large, glowing crystal. It was beautiful and terrifying. A cloaked figure stood before the crystal, their back to them. A feeling of dread washed over Elara. This was the source of the power she had been feeling.

A swirling vortex of shadows appeared in the clearing. It was a hungry, empty void. Whispers came from it, promising power and secrets if Elara would step inside. The air grew cold and the ground shook. Elara closed her eyes, trying to block out the terrifying sight and the tempting voices.

The whispers in Elara's mind grew stronger. The pendant around her neck felt like a shield, pushing back against the strange thoughts. The cave walls began to glow. Was she alone, or was something else in the cave with her, drawn by her pendant's power?

In an arena, Elara faced her next opponent, a huge and brutal fighter known as the 'Mountain of Might'. She knew she could not beat him with pure strength. She had to be smart and quick. The fight began, a dance of brute force against nimble evasion.

Elara and Kaelen found a chamber with a massive, glowing stone in the center, called the Heartstone. The runes on it glowed intensely. As Elara reached for it, a voice spoke in her mind. *You have come. The path has led you here.*

The whispers grew louder, speaking of old betrayals and a hidden destiny. Elara pressed on through the twisted forest. She felt like she was being watched from the shadows. Was it real, or was her tired mind playing tricks on her?

A monster attacked, and Kaelen created a shimmering barrier to protect them, but it was cracking. Elara realized the ancient stones around them could disrupt the creature's power. "The magic is still here!" she yelled. "We have to amplify it!" They worked together in a desperate attempt to channel the stones' energy and stop the monster.

The magical stone felt alive in Elara's hands. It began to show a vision of an ancient ritual. The vision expanded, filling the cavern around them. The air grew cold, and a low growl echoed from the shadows. A pair of evil green eyes appeared. The magic wasn't just showing them the past; it had brought something with it.

The shadow creature attacked. Kaelen's sword passed right through it. "It's not solid!" he yelled. "We can't fight it head-on!"

Elara saw that the stone was the creature's anchor to their world. "I have to break the connection!" she cried. She focused her own magic on the stone, trying to calm the chaotic energy. The creature shrieked as its body flickered.

The creature dissolved into smoke as Elara poured her will into the stone. The creature was gone, but the stone was now cold and cracked. Kaelen and Elias rushed to Elara's side. She was pale and leaned against the stone for support.

"Are you alright?" Kaelen asked.

Elara nodded weakly. "The stone is dormant. But before it faded, I felt a message." She looked at them with urgency. "It spoke of a 'Child of the Crimson Pendant,' and a 'rising storm.' It said the child is the key."

Kaelen looked confused, but Elias's eyes widened in recognition. "The Crimson Pendant..." he breathed. "It's not a legend. My research shows it was a real artifact, belonging to the Alchemist King. If there is a child with that pendant, they would be the king's heir."

"Then it's real," Elara said, her voice gaining strength. "And we have to find this child. The vision made it clear: they are in danger, and they are at the center of this."

Meanwhile, Anirudh was at the Kanjiram Academy. He felt like an outsider. The other students looked down on him, and the teachers seemed more interested in controlling him than helping him.

A tall, calm man approached him. "My name is Arjun," he said. "I can offer you a different path. A great storm is rising, and your destiny is tied to it. The answers you seek are not here. Come with me."

Anirudh looked at the academy walls, then back at the stranger. Arjun's words spoke to a deep need in his heart. He nodded. "I'll come."

Scene 1: The Whispering Scrolls

Arjun led Anirudh to a hidden vault in a mountain temple. Inside, magical scrolls floated in the air. "These are the Whispering Scrolls," Arjun said. "They will answer you if you're ready."

Anirudh touched a scroll. It dissolved into a rush of energy that flowed into his mind. He saw visions of great warriors in amazing battles.

The scroll's knowledge was imprinted on Anirudh's mind. He saw warriors who could move like water, stand strong as a mountain, or disappear like the wind. The visions were overwhelming, but they felt strangely familiar. The scroll was gone, but its knowledge was now a part of him.

Anirudh stumbled back, his head swimming with the knowledge of a thousand lifetimes of martial arts. The visions were chaotic, but they had planted the seeds of great power within him.

Scene 2: Animal Trials Begin

Arjun led Anirudh to the Forest of Shadows. "The scrolls opened your mind," Arjun said. "Now you must face the physical forms of that power. The Animal Trials begin."

A creature of pure fire, a Flame Serpent, appeared before them. "You must face it alone," Arjun said. "Remember what the scrolls taught you." Anirudh felt fear, but also determination. He turned to face the fiery snake.

Anirudh then faced the Ice Tiger. Remembering the lessons of the scrolls, he did not meet the tiger's force with his own. Instead, he moved like water, letting the tiger's attacks flow around him. He used precise strikes to disrupt the tiger's energy. He was learning that true strength was not just power, but control.

Anirudh used the "wind steps" to dodge the Flame Serpent's fire attacks. Then, using the water style from the scrolls, he created a shield of water to block the serpent's fire. He then shot a focused stream of water at the serpent, defeating it.

Act 2, Scene 3: The Tournament of Rising Suns

Anirudh entered the Grand Tournament. His first opponent was Garo, a monk famous for his speed with two daggers. As the fight began, Garo was a blur of steel. Anirudh centered himself. He used a special footwork technique to predict Garo's moves before he made them.

His next opponent was Vana, The Sound Bender. She used sound to twist his senses, making the ground feel like it was moving. Anirudh remembered Arjun's lesson: "Find the stillness within." He closed his eyes and focused, shutting out the noise. He could now sense the source of Vana's power.

Vana's sound attacks were powerful. Anirudh felt like he was fighting blind. Vana seemed to know his every move, stopping him with blasts of sound before he could get close.

Anirudh closed his eyes and found a calm center within himself. The sound attacks were still there, but they no longer confused him. He began to predict Vana's moves not by sight or sound, but by sensing the energy she used. He was no longer just reacting; he was predicting.

Anirudh easily dodged Garo's fast attacks. When Garo made a wide, sweeping attack, Anirudh saw his chance. He moved in and with a single, swift strike, he hit Garo's wrist, making him drop a blade. A second palm strike sent Garo out of the ring. Anirudh had won.

With his mind clear, Anirudh could anticipate Vana's sound attacks. She was forced to fight him up close, where Anirudh had the advantage. He defeated her with his superior hand-to-hand skill.

Scene 4: First Major Loss

His next opponent was Rudrak, The Thunder Heir. He was said to be unbeatable. Anirudh felt a fear he had not felt before. Rudrak was a mountain of a man, crackling with energy. His first punch smashed the ground where Anirudh had just been. Anirudh dodged, but he knew this would be his hardest fight.

Rudrak's power was too much for Anirudh. He tried to use his new skills, but Rudrak was like a wall of raw power. Arjun watched from the shadows. He knew that a true warrior must taste defeat to understand their own limits.

Anirudh's defenses were breaking under Rudrak's powerful attacks. He tried to find a weakness, but there was none. Each blocked punch sent shockwaves up his arms. He was focused only on surviving.

Rudrak gathered the storm around him. "Thunder Fist!" he roared, and brought his fist down with crushing force. Anirudh tried to block it, but the impact threw him across the arena. He hit the wall and everything went dark. From the shadows, Arjun whispered, "He must fall… to rise beyond."

Scene 5: The Aftermath & Reflection

Anirudh woke up in the healing chamber, his body aching. The pain of his first true loss was sharp. He had grown so much, but it was not enough. He wondered if he was destined to live in the shadow of his father's great name.

Lying on the cot, Anirudh thought about the fight. He was given an old, incomplete scroll called “To Rise, First Die.” It described a resurrection technique, but the most important parts were missing. Frustration grew inside him. "Am I just my father’s shadow?" he asked himself.

The incomplete scroll felt like a cruel joke. Rudrak's power had been absolute. Every skill Anirudh had learned had failed. He felt lost, wondering if he could ever be more than a pale copy of the great warriors he admired.

Scene 6: Village in Peril

News came that wild beasts were attacking his hometown. These were not normal animals, but creatures twisted by dark magic. "This is your test, Anirudh," Arjun said. "Not in an arena, but in the real world. Where lives are at stake." Anirudh felt a new purpose. He ran to save his home.

Anirudh arrived to find his village in chaos. The mutated beasts were strong and villagers were panicking. This was real danger, not a controlled fight in a tournament.

Anirudh understood Arjun's words. This was a real test. He had to use his skills and his judgment to save his people. He left the temple and headed towards the chaos in his village.

Act 2, Scene 7: Rebirth of Will

After helping his village, Anirudh sat in a mountain cave with the incomplete scroll. The real-world danger had cleared his mind. He looked at the scroll with new eyes. He realized that in the fight, he had not used one style, but blended all of them together. He was not meant to copy others, but to create something new, something that was his own.

He spent days in the cave, thinking about the scrolls and the animal trials. He realized the incomplete scroll, "To Rise, First Die," was not about a literal death. It was about the death of his old self, the one who was defined by his father and his teacher. He needed to create his own style.

Anirudh stood in the cavern. The sting of his defeat against Rudrak was still there, but the fear he felt protecting his village had given him a new clarity. The missing pieces of the scroll no longer felt like a problem, but like an invitation to create his own path.

Anirudh held the incomplete scroll. The visions from the other scrolls and the lessons from the animal trials came to his mind. The missing piece of the scroll was not a lost technique, but a new one he had to create himself. A new form. His form.

Scene 8: The Master's Acknowledgment

INT. HIDDEN DOJO - SUNSET

Anirudh stood before Arjun. Years of hard training had led to this moment. Arjun looked at him with new respect. He saw that Anirudh was no longer just a student.

“Not surpassed,” Anirudh replied calmly. “Only accepted who I am.”

Arjun understood. He lowered his head, not as a master to a student, but as an equal. It was a silent sign of Anirudh's great achievement.

Arjun bowed, a sign of respect from one warrior to another. Anirudh felt a quiet confidence. He was no longer trying to be someone else. He was on his own path.

Scene 9: The Invitation

In the quiet of the dojo, as Anirudh and Arjun shared a moment of mutual respect, a messenger bird with shimmering feathers flew through the open window. It carried a scroll sealed with the crest of the War Council of Realms.

Anirudh's heart pounded as he opened it. It was a formal invitation to the grand council. The same council where his father had been betrayed.

He looked at Arjun, his eyes full of questions. "This is a trap," Anirudh said, his voice low.

"Perhaps," Arjun replied, his face calm. "Or it is an opportunity. The truth you seek about your father may be there. But the council is a nest of vipers. Your strength will be tested in ways you cannot imagine."

Anirudh thought of his journey. The trials, the defeat, the battle for his village. He was no longer the angry orphan seeking revenge. He was a warrior who had found his own strength, his own purpose. He would not be defined by his father's shadow or his enemies' plans.

A fire lit in his eyes, not of rage, but of pure resolve. "Then I will go," he said. "I will face them, not as the son of a fallen king, but as myself. I will learn the truth."

Arjun nodded, a faint, proud smile on his lips. "Then your final trial begins now. You must prepare for a different kind of war. A war of words, of alliances, and of hidden daggers. Go, Anirudh. Your journey awaits."

Act 3: The Council of Shadows

The journey to the council was long. Anirudh traveled through bustling cities and quiet villages, seeing the world beyond his small town and the secluded temple. He saw the effects of the rising storm Arjun had spoken of – fear, uncertainty, and the growing power of dark forces.

Meanwhile, Elara, Kaelen, and the scholar Elias were on their own journey. Following the clues from Elias's research, they traveled towards the last known location of the Alchemist King's hidden workshop, hoping to find a way to track the child of the prophecy – Anirudh.

War of Power

Section 1: Shadows Before the Storm

Anirudh began his long journey to the council, his spirit forged and his purpose clear. Traveling with him was Meera, a young woman from his village who had insisted on accompanying him. She was quiet and observant, with sharp eyes that seemed to miss nothing, and she carried herself with a stillness that was unusual for someone so young. Anirudh found her presence calming, a quiet anchor in the storm of his thoughts.

Their journey took them through lands already touched by a growing darkness. They saw villages fortified with nervous guards and towns where whispers of war were on everyone's lips. The world felt like it was holding its breath.

They finally arrived at the War Council of Realms, a grand summit held in a fortress carved into the heart of a mountain. The air within was thick with tension. The three major factions were present: the stoic and disciplined Martial Clans, the mystical and secretive Alchemy Order, and the dangerous, power-hungry Shadow Fction.

Anirudh felt the weight of hundreds of eyes on him as he entered the council chamber. He was an unknown, an outsider, and his presence was a disruption.

A mysterious figure moved between the delegates, their face hidden by a deep cowl. They were known only as The Whisperer. With carefully chosen words, they stoked the fires of mistrust and ambition, pushing the factions closer to open war. The Whisperer's voice was like honeyed poison, and the leaders of the great factions seemed to drink it in, their own greed and fear making them easy puppets.

That night, Arjun found Anirudh looking out over the moonlit valley. "Be careful, Anirudh," the master warned, his voice low. "This is the same place where your father, Raghav, was betrayed. The tensions were the same. A manipulator worked from the shadows, just like this Whisperer. History is repeating itself. Do not trust anyone here."

Section 2: The First Ambush

On the road from the council, Anirudh’s convoy was ambushed by Shadow Assassins. They moved like ghosts, their attacks draining the energy from their victims. Their leader was Kavrik, a man with a face scarred by void energy and eyes that burned with a cold, dark fire. He was a master of void-style combat, a forbidden art that consumed life itself.

The assassins targeted Meera. As they closed in, a brilliant silver light erupted from her, knocking them back. She was a Moonblood Healer, a member of a rare lineage hunted by the Shadow Faction for their unique powers. Anirudh, stunned but resolute, fought to protect her.

He faced Kavrik, but the man's void-style was too powerful. Anirudh was defeated, left with a chilling warning from Kavrik: “Your bloodline will burn first.”

Section 3: Training in the Ruins

Arjun took the wounded Anirudh to the Ruins of Vajradan, an ancient city where his father had once trained. "To defeat the void, you must understand it," Arjun said. "And to understand it, you must face death."

He led Anirudh to a chamber that could simulate death-like conditions, forcing a warrior to unlock their latent power or be consumed. Inside, Anirudh's mind was pulled into a vision of his father's final battle, seeing firsthand the betrayal at the council.

He emerged from the chamber, not just alive, but transformed. He had gained the Vayu Vajra Ascendant Form, a powerful evolution of his style that crackled with golden energy. But the power came at a great cost, putting an immense strain on his body.

Section 4: The Siege of Kanjiram Academy

The Shadow Faction, emboldened by their success, launched a direct assault on the Kanjiram Academy, unleashing corrupted wild beasts onto the campus. The battle was fierce. Anirudh, now far more powerful, fought on the front lines, his Ascendant Form a golden beacon of hope against the tide of darkness. He single-handedly defeated a Flamehorn Rhino, a beast that had broken the academy's main gate.

Meera, her healing powers now revealed, saved countless lives, her silver light a shield against the shadows. Arjun was a whirlwind of destruction, dueling three of the Shadow Faction's top generals at once. But the enemy had an ace: the Obsidian Direwolf, a Beast King of immense power, which cornered Anirudh alone in the main courtyard.

Section 5: Duel with the Direwolf

The duel was legendary. The Direwolf was a creature of shadow and moonlight, cloaking itself in darkness and absorbing energy. Anirudh, pushed to his absolute limit, began to weave alchemical seals into his attacks, a skill inherited from his father that he had never used before. The seals countered the beast's regeneration, evening the odds.

For the final blow, Anirudh gambled everything. He used the incomplete Death-Resurrection Scroll technique, channeling his own life force into one, final, devastating attack. The Vayu Vajra struck the Direwolf with the force of a falling star, and the beast was consumed by golden light.

Anirudh collapsed, his heart stopping. He had won, but at the cost of his own life. For several minutes, he was technically dead, until Meera, with her powerful Moonblood healing, managed to revive him, her tears falling on his still face.

Section 6: Secrets Unveiled

After the battle, as Anirudh recovered, Arjun finally told him the whole truth. Anirudh’s father, Raghav, was not just a great warrior, but the Alchemist King, betrayed by a close friend within the War Council. Meera's family were sworn guardians of the king's bloodline, which was why she had been sent to find and protect him.

And the most shocking reveal: Kavrik, the master of the void who had almost killed him, was once Raghav's best friend and most trusted ally. Anirudh, his heart filled with a new, cold resolve, vowed to find the traitor and end the Shadow Faction's threat for good.

Section 7: The Gathering Storm

Word of the siege at Kanjiram spread like wildfire. The great factions, manipulated by The Whisperer and shocked by the Shadow Faction's brazen attack, began to muster their armies. A final battle was inevitable, to be held in the desolate Valley of Blood Moons.

Anirudh and his allies began to train with a new intensity. He worked on mastering his Ascendant Form and developing counter-attacks for Kavrik's void style. In a quiet moment before the war, he and Meera finally confessed their feelings for each other, a small moment of peace in a world about to be torn apart.

The final scene shows the armies of the allied factions marching towards the valley, the sky above them lit by two ominous, blood-red moons. The final war was about to begin.

Act 4 – The Eve of Red Moons

Sequence 1: The March of Armies

EXT. PLAINS BEFORE THE VALLEY – NIGHT

The world was painted in shades of blood and shadow. Above the vast, sweeping plains, the twin moons hung like weeping sores in the night sky, their crimson light bathing the land in an unnatural, ominous glow. A bitter wind swept down from the volcanic peaks lining the valley, carrying with it a fine, choking ash that tasted of sulfur and endings. It was a sky that promised no dawn.

Below, a river of fire flowed across the dying land. It was a river of men and women, an alliance of necessity forged in the face of a common, existential threat. Three distinct currents made up this river, marching as one yet separate, their banners stark against the gloom.

First were the Martial Clans, a legion of disciplined warriors clad in obsidian-black steel, their movements a symphony of synchronized purpose. Their banner, a golden sunburst on a field of night, seemed to defy the oppressive darkness. They were the unshakeable shield of the realms, their faces grim, their silence as heavy as the war hammers on their backs.

Beside them marched the Alchemy Order, a more unsettling sight. They moved not with the rigid discipline of soldiers, but with a fluid, almost silent grace. Silver flames danced on their banners, and a faint, metallic scent clung to the air around them, a strange perfume of minerals and magic. Their eyes, visible beneath deep cowls, seemed to watch the world in a way others could not, seeing the energies that bound and unmade all things.

And then there was the Shadow Faction. They were not so much an army as a tide of darkness given form. They moved in near-total silence, their black moon banners seeming to absorb the already faint light. They were a necessary evil, a darkness to fight darkness, and their presence sent a chill through the hearts of even their allies.

At the very head of this great host, walking with a solitude that no crowd could breach, was Anirudh. The Vayu Vajra, the Wind Thunder Form he had forged from his own pain and perseverance, was a quiet storm contained within him. A faint, golden energy, almost invisible, crackled around his skin, a stark contrast to the oppressive red light of the moons. The weight of a thousand expectations—from his master, from the memory of a father he never knew, from the hopeful and fearful eyes of the soldiers behind him—pressed down on him. This was the valley where his destiny would be forged, or shattered.

Further back, amidst the rows of nervous soldiers, Meera was already at work. While others sharpened their blades, she mended the broken. The volcanic ash fell on everyone, a prelude to the bloodshed to come, causing racking coughs and stinging eyes. With a calm that seemed to be a well of light in the encroaching darkness, she moved from soldier to soldier, her hands glowing with the soft, silver light of her Moonblood healing. She offered not just a cure for their physical ailments, but a moment of peace, a quiet reassurance that even here, on the brink of hell, there was still compassion.

As the armies made their final halt before the entrance to the Valley of Blood Moons, Arjun appeared at Anirudh’s side, his face a mask of grim resolve carved from the shadows. He placed a hand on Anirudh’s shoulder, his grip firm, grounding. He did not offer words of comfort or encouragement. He offered only the stark, unvarnished truth of the moment.

“Look around you, Anirudh,” Arjun said, his voice low, almost lost in the wind. “This is the crucible. The same forces that gathered to betray your father now gather to follow you. The board is set, the pieces are moving.” He turned to face Anirudh, his eyes boring into him. “Tonight, you will either surpass your father… or you will join him. There is no other path.”

Sequence 2: First Blood

The moment before the battle was a held breath, a silence stretched so taut it was louder than any war cry. Then, the horns blew, a gut-wrenching sound that tore the silence apart, and the two armies surged forward. For a heartbeat, the charge seemed to happen in slow motion. The front lines of the Martial Clans, a wall of disciplined steel, met the frenzied charge of the enemy's vanguard. The clash was a deafening roar of splintering shields, screaming metal, and the wet, percussive thud of bodies breaking. The battlefield, which had been a field of tense anticipation, erupted into a maelstrom of chaotic, brutal violence.

The initial clash was only a prelude. From the enemy's flank, a new horror emerged. Corrupted beasts, their forms twisted mockeries of nature, surged onto the field. They were creatures of nightmare, with matted fur, extra limbs, and eyes that burned with a malevolent green light. They tore through the allied lines, their claws and fangs ripping through armor as if it were paper. The organized battle lines faltered, threatening to break into a full-blown rout.

"They're trying to collapse our flank!" Anirudh yelled, his voice cutting through the din. He knew he couldn't command this battle from the rear. He was a weapon, and he needed to be pointed at the greatest threat. With a sharp gesture, he gathered a small, elite team of warriors. "With me! We cut the head off this beast!" He broke from the main force, a golden comet streaking towards the chaos.

In the heart of the battlefield, a different kind of light shone. Meera had created a sanctuary in the midst of the slaughter. A circle of soft, silver light pulsed around her, a protective ward that seemed to hum with a gentle power. Inside this Moonblood Circle, wounded soldiers found their strength returning, their wounds closing, their fear giving way to a renewed hope. It was an island of life in an ocean of death, and Meera was its unwavering lighthouse.

Anirudh and his team crashed into the pack of corrupted beasts, a whirlwind of steel and golden energy. But as he fought, a new threat emerged from the shadows. It was Kavrik's lieutenant, a hulking figure named Vorlag the Void-touched. He wielded a spear that seemed to be carved from solidified night, its tip weeping a black energy that hissed as it touched the air.

Vorlag pointed his void-spear at Anirudh. "The son of the fallen king," he rasped, his voice like grinding stone. "Your father's weakness runs in your veins."

He lunged, and Anirudh parried with his staff. The moment the void-spear touched the wood, the staff dissolved into black dust. Anirudh leaped back, his eyes wide. His primary weapon was gone.

Vorlag pressed his attack, his void-spear a blur of deadly strikes. Anirudh was forced to dodge and weave, his movements a desperate dance of survival. He was outmatched, disarmed, and facing a weapon that could unmake matter itself.

But Anirudh had more than just his martial skill. He had his father's knowledge. As Vorlag lunged for a final, killing blow, Anirudh slapped his own chest, his fingers tracing a complex, glowing seal. A wave of golden energy washed over his skin, hardening it with an alchemical seal just as the void-spear struck.

The spear, which should have passed through him, screeched against his hardened skin, the sound of tearing reality. The impact threw Anirudh back, his body screaming in protest, but he was alive. The seal had held.

Vorlag stared in disbelief, his attack thwarted. It was the only opening Anirudh needed. He surged forward, his hand crackling with the golden energy of the Vayu Vajra, and struck Vorlag's chest in a single, devastating blow. The Void-touched warrior crumpled, his armor shattering, the light in his eyes extinguished.

Anirudh stood, breathing heavily, his body bruised and battered. He had won, but only just. The victory was a stark reminder of the power he was up against. This was just the beginning.

Sequence 3: Duel on the Blood Moon Cliffs

High above the chaotic battlefield, on the jagged cliffs overlooking the valley, Kavrik waited. He stood silhouetted against the bleeding moons, the wind whipping his dark cloak around him. In his hands, he held twin shadow blades that seemed to drink the crimson light, dripping with an inky blackness that was not liquid, but pure, condensed void.

Anirudh, leaving the skirmish below in the capable hands of his team, scaled the cliffs with a speed that defied gravity. He arrived on the precipice, his heart pounding not from the climb, but from the confrontation he knew was coming. This was the man who had haunted his steps, the man who wore his father's friendship like a stolen cloak.

KAVRIK

“Your father chose honor over victory. He believed in mercy, in second chances. Look where it got him. Will you make the same mistake?”

ANIRUDH

“My father chose to trust a friend. That wasn't a mistake. The mistake was his friend's betrayal. I won't choose honor or victory. I’ll choose truth over both.”

The duel began with a clash that sent sparks of golden light and black void into the night. It was a battle of impossible speed. Kavrik's blades were a blur, leaving trails of un-light in their wake, each strike aimed with lethal precision. Anirudh, armed with a new staff of hardened ironwood, moved like the wind, his Vayu Vajra form a whirlwind of defensive blocks and lightning-fast counters.

But Kavrik was a master of the void, and he was not fighting fair. With a guttural chant, he summoned Shadow Doppelgangers, spectral copies of himself that emerged from the rock and shadows. They were perfect replicas, each armed with the same deadly blades, and they pressed the attack on Anirudh from all sides.

Overwhelmed, Anirudh was forced to give ground. He was fighting a war on multiple fronts, his senses screaming, his body pushed to its absolute limit. He knew he could not win a conventional fight. He had to embrace the lesson he had learned in the Ruins of Vajradan. He had to face death.

As the doppelgangers closed in for the kill, Anirudh took a desperate gamble. He activated the forbidden Death-Resurrection Style, a technique he had only glimpsed in his visions. His life force flared, his body erupting in a blinding explosion of golden energy. The doppelgangers dissolved into smoke, and Kavrik himself was thrown back.

But the cost was immense. Anirudh's body was wracked with pain, his vision blurring. Kavrik, seeing his chance, unleashed his own forbidden art, the Eternal Eclipse. The world went black, the red moons vanishing, the air growing impossibly cold. It was a technique that attacked the senses, the mind, the very soul.

In the suffocating darkness, Anirudh fought on pure instinct. He could not see, he could not hear, but he could feel Kavrik's hatred, a burning beacon in the void. He let his Vayu Vajra guide him, his final strike a desperate, brilliant flash of golden lightning in the eternal night. There was a scream of pain, and the darkness shattered. Kavrik stood disarmed, his shadow blades lying broken on the ground, his chest scorched by Anirudh's attack.

But he was not defeated. With a sneer, Kavrik dissolved into a mist of shadows, his voice echoing in the wind. “You have won nothing, boy. The council room is where your blood will spill. You cannot escape your own destiny.”

Sequence 4: The Serpent in the Council

The victory was a hollow one. The enemy forces were in retreat, but the cost had been high. The allied leaders gathered in the main war council tent, a tense and somber atmosphere hanging in the air. As the reports of the victory came in, a few weak cheers erupted. But the celebration was short-lived.

Lord Varash, the stoic and respected head of the Alchemy Order, stepped into the center of the tent. His face was a mask of cold fury. He pointed a trembling finger at Anirudh. "We have a traitor in our midst!" he boomed, his voice silencing the tent. "This boy... this 'hero'... is a shadow infiltrator! He fights with forbidden techniques, he consorts with darkness. He is the reason we have suffered such losses!"

A wave of shock and confusion washed over the council. Before anyone could react, the tent flaps flew open, and a dozen hidden assassins, their faces covered, struck from the shadows. They were not the enemy's soldiers. Their armor bore the crest of the Alchemy Order.

The tent erupted into chaos. The assassins moved with deadly precision, their targets the other leaders of the council. In the heart of the betrayal stood Lord Varash, a cruel smile on his face. "Your father was a fool, just like you," he sneered at Anirudh. "He wanted to share the secrets of alchemy, to limit its power. I, on the other hand, understand its true potential. I orchestrated his death, and now I will control both the alchemy of the Order and the beast power you so foolishly wield."

Arjun, who had been standing beside Anirudh, moved with the speed of a striking viper. He threw himself in front of Anirudh, his sword a blur, deflecting a dozen poisoned blades meant for his student. But he was outnumbered. A half-dozen more blades found their mark, and Arjun fell, his body riddled with wounds, a pained gasp on his lips.

Seeing his master fall, something inside Anirudh broke. The grief, the rage, the betrayal... it was too much. The golden light of the Vayu Vajra turned dark, shot through with veins of raw, untamed energy. The power of the wild beasts he had fought, the power he had tried to control, now surged to the surface. His body contorted, his form shifting, growing. He was no longer just human. He was a storm. He was a beast. He was the Stormbeast, and his roar of pure, untamed fury tore the tent, and the world, apart.

Sequence 5: The Stormbeast's Fury

The Stormbeast was a creature of pure, elemental rage. Lightning arced from its body, tearing the war council tent to shreds and sending the assassins flying. The sky, which had been clear moments before, was now a roiling mass of black clouds, a reflection of the storm in Anirudh's soul. The ground shook with his every step, and the wild beasts that had been driven back from the battlefield now felt a new call, a new master. They turned, their eyes glowing with a renewed frenzy, and charged back towards the camp, drawn to the raw power of the Stormbeast.

Lord Varash, however, was prepared. He held up a hand, and in his palm was a stone that pulsed with a malevolent crimson light. The Crimson Philosopher's Stone. It was an artifact of immense power, created through forbidden rituals, and it gave Varash the power to match the Stormbeast blow for blow. He encased himself in a shield of hardened earth and fought back with blasts of raw, chaotic energy.

The battle was a cataclysm. The camp was torn apart, the ground scorched, the air thick with the smell of ozone and blood. Anirudh, lost in the heart of the storm, was no longer fighting for justice or for his father's legacy. He was fighting out of pure, animalistic rage, his humanity buried under a mountain of pain. He was losing himself, becoming the monster Varash had accused him of being.

Then, through the chaos, a single, clear voice cut through the roar of the storm. "Anirudh!"

It was Meera. She stood at the edge of the destruction, her face pale, her eyes wide with fear, but she did not run. She walked into the heart of the storm, her silver light a fragile shield against the raging elements. She reached out a hand, not to fight him, but to touch him. "Anirudh, please," she whispered, her voice filled with a desperate plea. "Come back. This is not you."

Her voice, her touch, her unwavering belief in him... it was an anchor in the storm. The rage in Anirudh's heart faltered. The Stormbeast roared, not in fury, but in pain, in confusion. He was fighting a new battle now, not against Varash, but against the beast within. For a moment, it seemed he would be lost forever. But then, slowly, painfully, the storm began to recede. The lightning faded, the clouds parted, and Anirudh, still in his monstrous form but with his own eyes looking out, fell to his knees.

Sequence 6: A Father's Legacy, A Son's Choice

The duel was not over. Varash, seeing Anirudh weakened, pressed his attack. The battle moved from the ruined camp to the cliffs overlooking the valley, the world below a sea of fire and shadow. Varash, his face twisted in a triumphant sneer, taunted Anirudh with his father's memory.

"Your father begged before I crushed his heart," he spat, his voice dripping with venom. "He pleaded for his life, for you. He was weak, just like you are now."

The words were meant to drive Anirudh back into a blind rage, to make him lose control again. But they had the opposite effect. They gave him clarity. He was not his father. He would not be defined by his father's death. He would be defined by his own choices.

He rose to his feet, the Stormbeast form still clinging to him, but it was no longer a cage of rage. It was a weapon, and he was in control. He looked at Varash, not with hatred, but with a cold, clear resolve. And then, he did something unexpected. He began to trace a series of complex seals in the air, the same seals his father had used, the Three Seal Harmony Technique. But he wove them together with the raw, elemental power of his Stormbeast form, creating something new, something that was both his father's legacy and his own unique strength.

The three seals—one for earth, one for air, one for fire—hummed with an immense power, amplified by the storm within him. He was not just controlling the elements. He was them. For the final blow, he did not attack Varash directly. He channeled the full, unbridled fury of the storm, a torrent of pure lightning, into the Crimson Philosopher's Stone in Varash's hand.

The stone, which had been Varash's source of power, now became his undoing. It could not contain the raw, elemental energy Anirudh poured into it. It began to crack, to glow with an unstable, blinding light. Varash screamed, a sound of pure terror, as the stone overloaded. With a final, deafening explosion that lit up the night sky, the Crimson Philosopher's Stone shattered, and Lord Varash, the serpent in the council, was obliterated, consumed by the very power he had sought to control.

Sequence 7: A New Dawn, A Lingering Shadow

The battle was over. The sun, a pale and hesitant visitor, began to rise, its light touching a world scarred and broken. The remnants of the Shadow Faction, their leaders defeated, scattered to the winds. The valley, which had been a place of death and betrayal, was now quiet.

Arjun survived, but the cost of his bravery was high. The poisoned blades of the assassins had left him permanently crippled, his days as a warrior over. He passed his sword, the one he had carried for a lifetime, to Anirudh, not as a master to a student, but as a father to a son. "It is yours now," he said, his voice weak but firm. "Lead them."

Weeks later, under a calm and peaceful moon, Anirudh and Meera stood before a simple, unmarked grave on a windswept hill. It was Raghav's grave. Anirudh placed a hand on the cool stone, a sense of peace finally settling in his heart. He had avenged his father, not with blind rage, but with a strength that was uniquely his own.

His voice was quiet, but it carried the weight of his long journey as he spoke, not to Meera, but to the world, to the memory of his father, to himself. "Power is not what I inherited," he said. "It’s what I choose to protect."

Meera took his hand, her presence a quiet promise of a shared future. As they stood there, the camera pulled back, back over the valley, over the mountains, to the far-off horizon. And there, in the distance, a colossal silhouette moved against the rising sun, a beast of unimaginable size, a slumbering titan awakened by the echoes of the battle. The war was over, but a new saga was just beginning.
